Syllabus

Climate Change Governance: Science, Data & Models · Fall 2025–26

Course Description

Short version: This interdisciplinary course equips students with the tools, models, and frameworks to understand and influence climate, water, energy, and food policy through data-driven systems thinking. It blends technical modeling with governance insights into climate adaptation, sustainable agriculture, and infrastructure resilience. Students engage in scenario-based planning, explore socio-technical systems, and work with real-world case studies from the Indus Basin.

Long version: This interdisciplinary course equips students with the tools, models, and frameworks necessary to understand and influence climate, water, energy, and food policy through data-driven systems thinking. It blends technical modeling with governance insights into climate adaptation, sustainable agriculture, and infrastructure resilience. Students engage in scenario-based planning, explore socio-technical systems, and work with real-world case studies from the Indus Basin. Across fourteen weeks, the course moves from the physical science basis of climate change through the international and national policy landscape, into hands-on work with scenario tools and integrated assessment models across the energy, water, and agriculture sectors, culminating in a nexus-focused capstone project.

Course Learning Outcomes

CLOOutcome
CLO1Explain the physical drivers of climate change and key metrics (radiative forcing, carbon budgets, etc.).
CLO2Critically assess climate policy and reporting frameworks (NDCs, carbon pricing, mitigation/adaptation).
CLO3Apply computational modeling and data visualization tools to develop pathways for energy, water, and agriculture systems.
CLO4Interpret and communicate complex scenario results to technical and non-technical audiences.

Course Philosophy on AI Use

Use of generative AI tools (ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, etc.) is permitted and expected on all take-home work: assignments, project milestones, policy briefs. You may use AI freely for brainstorming, research, drafting, and refinement.

However, 80% of your grade is assessed synchronously or in-room, where AI use is impossible: writing sprints, discussion leading, the midterm exam, and the project defense. These components are where your actual understanding is tested.

We assume you use AI; we grade what remains when you do. The honor code here is simple: show up, think hard, engage live.
