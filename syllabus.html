Syllabus

Climate Change Governance: Science, Data & Models · Fall 2025–26

Course Description

Short version: Climate, water, energy, and food aren't separate problems — they're one system, and that's the only way this course treats them. You'll see how a shock in one sector ripples through the others, and build the judgment to reason across all of them at once. Starting from the physical foundations of climate science, you'll combine modeling and data-science tools with governance to work through real cases in climate adaptation, sustainable agriculture, and infrastructure resilience. The course ends with a group project: modeling an actual policy problem using real local and global data.

Long version: This course sits at the intersection of climate science, data analytics, and policy governance. You'll leave with the ability to build integrated scenarios using state-of-the-art modeling and data-visualization tools — and the judgment to know when a model is being trusted too far.

Each week starts with foundational readings and a lecture. You then apply that material in an in-class case study — scenario design, a policy intervention, a sectoral analysis. Across the semester that covers mitigation, adaptation, impacts, risk management, and resilience, in energy, water, agriculture, and the nexus where the three meet.

Across fourteen weeks, the course moves from the physical science basis of climate change through the international and national policy landscape, into hands-on work with scenario tools and integrated assessment models across the energy, water, and agriculture sectors, culminating in a nexus-focused capstone project.

Course Philosophy on AI Use

Use of generative AI tools (ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, etc.) is permitted and expected on all take-home work: assignments, project milestones, policy briefs. You may use AI freely for brainstorming, research, drafting, and refinement.

However, most of your grade is assessed synchronously or in-room, where AI use is impossible: quizzes, class participation, the midterm exam, the final exam, and your live project defense. These components are where your actual understanding is tested.

We assume you use AI; we grade what remains when you do. The honor code here is simple: show up, think hard, engage live.

Policies

Academic Honesty

LUMS has zero tolerance for academic dishonesty. You're responsible for upholding academic integrity — if you're unsure where a line is, check the student handbook or ask the instructor or TA before, not after. Essays can be checked for similarity via similarity@lums.edu.pk before submission.

Generative AI Policy

See Course Philosophy on AI Use above for how this plays out in practice. The university-level policy: AI tools are permitted for brainstorming, refining a research question, finding information, drafting an outline, and checking grammar and style. They are not permitted for submitting AI-generated content as your own in discussion posts or live sessions, for completing group work assigned specifically to you, or for writing any part of a graded submission — outline through final draft. Violations are treated as academic misconduct.

This class is a harassment-free zone. No behavior that makes someone uncomfortable or undermines their standing in the class will be tolerated. To report harassment experienced or observed, contact me directly, or OAI at oai@lums.edu.pk / harassment@lums.edu.pk.
