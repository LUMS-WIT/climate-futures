Climate Change Governance: Science, Data & Models

ENGG562 / ENV462 · Syed Babar Ali School of Science & Engineering, LUMS

  • Science The physical evidence — energy balance, carbon cycle, feedbacks — and why we're sure this one's on us.
  • Governance How the world actually decides what to do about it: treaties, NDCs, carbon pricing, the mechanisms that turn agreement into action.
  • Data Real datasets, not toy examples — emissions, water stress, crop yields — and the judgment to read them right.
  • Models The scenario tools and integrated assessment models that let you test a policy choice before anyone has to commit to it.

Climate, water, energy, and food aren't separate problems — treating them that way is how governments get the sequencing wrong. This course teaches you to see them as one system: how a shock in one sector ripples through the others, and how to reason across all of them at once. You'll start from the physical foundations of climate science, then pick up the modeling and data tools to work through real cases in adaptation, agriculture, and infrastructure resilience. It ends with a group project where you model an actual policy problem using real local and global data.

Why This Course Matters

The warming signature

Every place on Earth has its own warming pattern. Pakistan's is stark — the last twenty years are the warmest in the record. Here it is next to three other regions. Same direction, different speed.

What that looks like on the ground

  • 2010: monsoon floods hit Pakistan, displacing 20 million people and causing roughly $10 billion in damage. Read more
  • 2015: a heat wave in Karachi kills over a thousand people in a few days; the power grid buckles under demand. Read more
  • 2022: a third of Pakistan goes underwater, over 1,700 people die — then drought hits the same basin the following year. Read more
  • 2022: rivers across Europe run low enough to strand barges and idle power plants. Read more
  • 2020–2023: the Horn of Africa has its worst drought in four decades; tens of millions go short on food and water. Read more
  • 2023: wildfire seasons break records in Canada and Greece. Read more

One system, not five

Look again at that 2022 flood. Nearly 5 million acres of cropland went under in Sindh alone, and the province lost most of its expected cotton, rice, and sugarcane harvest for the year. That's not a water story or an agriculture story — it's both, at once, and it shows up next as a food-price story somewhere a thousand kilometers away. Study "water policy," "energy policy," and "agriculture policy" as three separate subjects, and you'll miss exactly the part that matters: the damage moves through the system, not around it.

That's why the decisions made this decade carry so much weight. A dam takes a decade to build and runs for a century. A crop subsidy shapes what farmers plant for a generation. Get the sequencing wrong now, and the people who inherit the consequences aren't the ones who made the call.

What you'll actually learn

  1. Read the physical evidence — IPCC reports, temperature and emissions data — and tell a solid finding from a shaky one.
  2. Follow how climate policy actually gets made — NDCs, carbon pricing, water treaties — and where it breaks down between ministries.
  3. Use real modeling tools (Scenario Explorer, integrated assessment models) to test what happens if a policy choice goes one way instead of another.
  4. Turn a complicated scenario into something a decision-maker can act on in one page.
  5. See energy, water, and food as one connected system instead of three separate briefs.

The hopeful part

None of this is unsolvable. Solar is now cheaper than coal in most markets. We know how to price carbon and water. Some countries have rebuilt their entire power system in under a decade. The tools exist.

What's still missing is people who can hold the whole system in their head, not just one sector, and turn that understanding into a decision someone can actually act on. That's the gap this course is built to close.
