Week Module Topic Readings Notes
Module 1 — Climate Science Foundations · Weeks 1–3

We start with the physics: how greenhouse gases trap heat, who worked that out, and how. By week 3 you should be able to read a temperature record and explain what's forcing it — not just recite that it's warming.

Learning objectives

  • Explain Earth's energy balance and the carbon cycle in your own words.
  • Trace the arc from Fourier's 1824 hypothesis to Hansen's 1988 testimony to CMIP7.
  • Read observational climate data and tell forcing, feedback, and noise apart.

Guiding question: How do we know it's us, and not natural variability?

Module 2 — Policy Landscape · Weeks 4–5

Science tells you what's happening. This module is about who decides what to do about it — and why that machinery moves so much slower than the physics does.

Learning objectives

  • Describe how energy, water, and land use (AFOLU) anchor the modern economy.
  • Explain the structure of the UNFCCC, the Paris Agreement, and NDCs.
  • Assess how the Enhanced Transparency Framework holds countries to their commitments.

Guiding question: If every country agrees warming is a problem, why is coordinated action still this hard?
4 M2 Anthropocene and the modern economy — energy, water, AFOLU Smil, Grand Transitions (Ch. 1–3); Gates, How to Avoid a Climate Disaster (Ch. 1–3) Project milestone — team formation
5 M2 International agreements — UNFCCC, Paris Agreement, NDCs, the Enhanced Transparency Framework; Climate Action Simulation, Game Session 1 🛠 📄 UNFCCC ETF Reference Manual (2022); CDKN (2016) NDC Quick-Start Guide Discussion: CDKN (2016) NDC Quick-Start Guide
Module 3 — Scenarios & Integrated Assessment · Weeks 6–8

Policy has to be tested before it's real. This module is where you learn to use the scenario tools researchers and governments use to do that — and where you learn their limits.

Learning objectives

  • Explain what an SSP narrative is and why modelers use scenario families instead of single forecasts.
  • Use an integrated assessment model to trace how a mitigation pathway plays out.
  • Connect carbon-pricing and fiscal mechanisms to national policy case studies.

Guiding question: A model is only as good as its assumptions — which assumptions in these models should you distrust?
6 M3 Use of scenarios in modelling and policy analysis — SSP narratives; Hands-on: Scenario Explorer 🛠 📄 IPCC AR6 WGIII Ch. 3: Mitigation pathways; O'Neill et al. (2017) Discussion: O'Neill et al. (2017) · Project milestone 1 — proposal due

Module 4 — Energy–Water–Agriculture Nexus · Weeks 9–13

Now you take it apart by sector, then put it back together. Energy, water, and agriculture each get their own systems and tools — then you couple them and see where the trade-offs actually live.

Learning objectives

  • Model energy-system trade-offs across decarbonization pathways.
  • Quantify water stress and demand at basin scale, and connect it to questions of equity.
  • Trace crop and land-use risk under a changing climate, and where it collides with food security.
  • Couple two or more sectors and identify where a gain in one creates a cost in another.

Guiding question: where in the Indus Basin would fixing one sector's problem quietly break another's?
